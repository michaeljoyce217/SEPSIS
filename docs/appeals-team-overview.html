Sepsis Appeal Engine

AI-Assisted Appeal Letter Generation

What It Does

When we receive a denial letter for a sepsis case, this system helps draft an appeal letter by automatically pulling together all the relevant information and generating a first draft.

You still review and approve every letter. The AI does the research and drafting; you provide the judgment and final approval.

Evidence Hierarchy: Notes vs Structured Data

The system uses a deliberate hierarchy when building appeal arguments. Understanding this hierarchy is critical for reviewing the generated letters.

PRIMARY EVIDENCE: Physician Notes

Clinical documentation represents the physician's medical judgment and interpretation of the patient's condition. This is the foundation of the appeal because it captures the clinical reasoning that justifies the diagnosis.

SUPPORTING EVIDENCE: Structured Data

Labs, vitals, and medication records provide objective measurements that corroborate what the physician documented. These support the clinical narrative but don't replace clinical judgment.

Why This Order Matters

Evidence Type What It Provides Example
Physician Notes
(Primary)		 Clinical interpretation and medical judgment - the "why" behind the diagnosis "Patient developed septic shock requiring vasopressor support to maintain adequate perfusion"
Structured Data
(Supporting)		 Objective measurements that back up the clinical interpretation MAP 58 mmHg at 14:00, Lactate 4.2 mmol/L, Norepinephrine started 14:30
Documented Diagnoses
(Supporting)		 Formal diagnosis descriptions from Epic to quote directly "Severe sepsis with septic shock due to E. coli urinary tract infection"

The appeal letter cites physician notes first, then uses structured data to provide the specific values and timestamps that support those clinical statements.

Conflicts Appendix: When Data Doesn't Match

The system automatically compares what the physician notes say against what the structured data shows. When there's a discrepancy, it flags this for your review.

What Is a Conflict?

A conflict occurs when the physician documentation states something that the objective data doesn't support. These require CDI review before the letter is sent.

Examples of Conflicts

CONFLICT: Note says "MAP maintained >65" but vitals show MAP values of 58, 61, 54 recorded during the same timeframe
CONFLICT: Note says "lactate normalized" but labs show lactate still elevated at 3.8 mmol/L at time of documentation
CONFLICT: Note says "no vasopressors needed" but medication records show norepinephrine administration

What to Do When You See Conflicts

CDI Reviewer Action Required

  • Review the specific times - Data from different times is not necessarily a conflict
  • Check for documentation timing - The note may have been written before/after the data in question
  • Verify the clinical context - There may be a valid explanation for the apparent discrepancy
  • Consider whether to proceed - If the conflict weakens the appeal, the letter may need revision
  • Query if needed - Some conflicts may warrant a physician query for clarification

The conflicts appendix appears before the letter body in the Word document, so you see these flags immediately when you open the file.

What Goes In

1. The Denial Letter

The PDF denial letter from the payor. The system reads this to understand what arguments we need to counter.

2. Past Winning Appeals (Gold Letters)

A library of our past successful appeals. The system finds the most similar past case and uses that winning letter as a template - proven arguments get reused.

3. Clinical Notes from Epic (PRIMARY)

The system pulls ALL notes from the encounter across 14 types:

  • Progress Notes, H&P, Discharge Summary
  • ED Notes, ED Triage, ED Provider Notes
  • Consults, Nursing Notes, Code Documentation
  • And more...

These form the primary evidence for the appeal.

4. Structured Data from Epic (SUPPORTING)

Objective clinical measurements that support the physician documentation:

  • Labs: Lactate, WBC, procalcitonin, cultures, creatinine, bilirubin, platelets
  • Vitals: Temperature, MAP, heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO2, GCS
  • Medications: Antibiotics, vasopressors, fluids with administration times
  • Diagnoses: Documented diagnosis descriptions (e.g., "Severe sepsis with septic shock due to MRSA")

This data is cross-checked against notes to detect conflicts.

5. Propel Criteria

The official clinical definition of sepsis that we use to justify the diagnosis.

What Comes Out

A Word Document with Three Parts:

  1. Strength Assessment - A score (1-10) rating how strong the appeal is, with notes on what evidence is present vs. missing
  2. Conflicts Appendix (if any) - Flags where physician notes don't match structured data, requiring your review before sending
  3. The Appeal Letter - A complete draft addressing the denial arguments with clinical evidence, citing notes first, then supporting data

How It Builds the Letter

1

Read the Denial

Extracts the payor's arguments and identifies what we need to counter.

2

Find a Similar Past Winner

Searches our library of past successful appeals to find the most similar case. Uses that winning letter as a template.

3

Gather Clinical Evidence

Pulls ALL notes from the encounter plus labs, vitals, meds, and diagnoses from Epic.

4

Check for Conflicts

Compares what the physician notes say vs. what the structured data shows. Flags any discrepancies for your review in the conflicts appendix.

5

Generate the Appeal

Writes the letter citing physician notes first as primary evidence, then structured data as support. Uses the winning template and Propel criteria.

6

Assess Strength

Evaluates the letter and scores what evidence is strong vs. what might be missing.

What You Need to Review

Every Letter Needs Your Approval

The AI generates a draft, but you:

  • Verify the clinical facts are accurate
  • Check that the arguments make sense for this case
  • Review any flagged conflicts before sending
  • Ensure the notes-first, data-second hierarchy makes sense
  • Edit as needed before sending

Special Attention: Conflicts Appendix

If the document has a "CONFLICTS DETECTED" section, stop and review these carefully. These indicate places where the structured data doesn't back up what's in the physician notes. You may need to:

  • Verify the timing of notes vs. data
  • Edit the letter to address the discrepancy
  • Query the physician for clarification
  • Decide whether to proceed with the appeal
